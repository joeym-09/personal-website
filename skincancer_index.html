Problem: Skin Cancer

One in every three cancer diagnosed is skin cancer. The prevalence of skin cancer can be attributed to the deplete of ozone levels. A 10% reduction in ozone levels is estimated to cause at least 345 000 new cases of skin cancer. Increase recreational sun exposure causes increased risk of skin cancer. Having more than 5 sunburns is predicted to double your risk for melanoma, a type of skin cancer. Additionally, skin cancer is easily misdiagnosed by practioners. Approximately 20% of cancer cases are misdiagnosed.

Dataset: MNIST HAM 10000

Different Classes of Skin Cancer

The dataset used to train the model consist of 10,015 labelled images of skin moles seperated into 7 different clasess of skin cancer.

Dermatofibromas

It's a type of noncancerous skin cancer that is usually harmless, and therefore no treatment is required.

Basal Cell Carcinoma

Basal Cell Carcinoma is a type of cancerous skin lesion that develops in the basal cell layer located in the lower part of the epidermis.

Melanoma

A type of malignant skin cancer that originates from melanocytes, cells that are responsible for the pigment of your skin.

Benign Keratosis

A type of noncancerous and slow-growing skin cancer. They can be left untreated as they are typically harmless.

Actinic Keratosis

Actinic Keratosis is a type of noncancerous, or benign skin cancer. However, if left untreated, it usually develops into squamous cell carcinoma (which is cancerous). It appears very rough like a scaly patch which is developed from prolong sun exposure.

Melanocytic Nevi

This is a type of benign melanocytic tumor. Patients with melanocytic nevi are considered to be at a higher risk of melanoma.

Vascular Lesions

This class of skin cancer is composed of a wide range of skin lesions including cherry angiomas, angiokeratomas, and pyogenic granulomas. They are similarly characterized as being red or purple in color and often appears as a raised bump.

Solution

Leveraging Convolutional Neural Networks

The potential for artificial intelligence to disrupt healthcare is incredible! In fact, artificial intelligence has the capacity to diagnose skin cancer with far better accuracy and much faster than human beings. I leveraged a specific type of machine learning algorithm, convolutional neural networks (CNNs), which is widely used in the area of image classification to detect skin cancer from pictures of moles. To decrease computational power needed to train the model, I used a pretrained convolutional neural network, Mobile Net which is trained on a dataset of 14 million images.
